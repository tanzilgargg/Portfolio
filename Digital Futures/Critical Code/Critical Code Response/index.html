Understanding the Problem

Platform Monopolies

Apple and Google control almost 100% of the smartphone operating system market in Canada. They force all app distribution through their stores and take 30% of every transaction. This "app tax" extracts billions from the Canadian economy annually, enriching foreign shareholders while local developers and communities struggle.

Anti-Circumvention Laws

Canadian copyright law makes it illegal to bypass digital locks, even for legal purposes like repairing your own devices or installing alternative software. These laws give manufacturers enormous control over products you supposedly own. You can't fix your phone, modify your tractor, or choose where to install appsâ€”not because it's technically impossible, but because it's illegal.

Surveillance Capitalism

Most digital platforms profit by extracting, analyzing, and selling your personal data. Your behavior, relationships, and private information become commodities. Platform monopolies make it nearly impossible to opt outâ€”you either accept surveillance or lose access to essential digital services.

Digital Sovereignty

Digital sovereignty means having meaningful control over your digital life and infrastructure. It means choosing your own software, controlling your own data, and supporting local digital economies instead of being locked into foreign platforms. This project imagines what that could look like.

Research Foundation

This speculative prototype is grounded in critical technology research and inspired by scholars and activists working on digital rights, platform regulation, and technological sovereignty.

Key Concepts

  • Enshittification: The gradual degradation of platforms as they prioritize profits over users (Cory Doctorow)
  • Right to Repair: The principle that consumers should be able to repair their own devices
  • Interoperability: The ability of different systems to work together without permission
  • Adversarial Interoperability: Making systems work together even when companies try to prevent it

Influenced By

  • Cory Doctorow's work on digital rights and platform regulation
  • Canadian right-to-repair advocacy
  • Platform economy critique (Nick Srnicek, Shoshana Zuboff)
  • Critical design and speculative futures (Dunne & Raby)
