Notion

Zara's mobile shopping experience is visually striking but usability challenges often result in poor product discoverability and user frustration. This case study explores how users interact with Zara's mobile site and identifies key improvements through user research and usability testing.

02

Problem Statement & Goals

Problem

Zara's users—especially mobile shoppers aged 18–34—struggle with browsing, filtering, and assessing product availability quickly. These issues lead to drop-offs before users reach the product detail or checkout pages.

"I can't tell if it's available in my size unless I click each item."

Business Impact

  • High bounce rates on mobile devices
  • Low mobile conversion rates
  • Decreased user satisfaction and trust

User-Centric Goal

Improve browsing efficiency and product discoverability on mobile devices for fashion-forward, time-constrained shoppers.

Methodologies

  • Quantitative Survey (n = 35)
  • Remote Usability Testing (5 participants)

Research Objectives

  • Understand how users search, filter, and decide on Zara's mobile site
  • Identify usability blockers and unmet expectations
  • Explore shopping behavior and needs of mobile-first users

Key Findings

Pain Points Identified

  • Filter Inconsistency: Filters reset or overlap; not mobile-optimized
  • Navigation Loops: Clicking a product often takes users back to the top, breaking their flow
  • Unclear Size Availability: Product sizes shown only after deep diving into each listing
  • No Product Comparison or Wishlist Tools: Users struggle to hold mental comparisons between items

User Quotes

"I can't tell if it's available in my size unless I click each item."
"Filters reset every time. It's annoying to start over."
"I scroll too much because I don't trust the categories."

05

Insights & Opportunities

Key Insights

  • Zara's mobile-first experience lacks tailored UX for mobile behavior patterns
  • Product uncertainty (fit, fabric, sizing) is a recurring theme
  • Purchase confidence is low due to missing contextual shopping support

Opportunities

Persistent Filters

A floating/sticky filter component on mobile would reduce cognitive load and help users refine searches progressively.

Preview Stock Status

A badge system showing "In Stock in Your Size" directly on product thumbnails could boost trust and reduce unnecessary clicks.

Comparison Tools

Help users make decisions faster by allowing them to compare 2–3 outfits or save options to review later.

Visual Promotions

Introduce banner tags like "On Sale" or "New Drop" at thumbnail level to highlight promotions and new arrivals.

06

Usability Testing

Test Details

  • Participants: 5 users
  • Tested Prototypes: Low-fidelity mobile wireframes
  • Focus Areas: Filter, wishlist, size badge concept

Test Tasks

  • Filter items by size and price
  • Compare two products
  • Identify sale items quickly

Key Feedback

  • Users liked sticky filters but wanted more spacing between touch targets
  • "In stock" badges were a hit: all 5 said it saved time
  • Wishlist icon placement was unclear—led to redesign
07

Reflection & Lessons Learned

This project deepened my understanding of how surface-level aesthetics can often mask usability issues. It reinforced the importance of testing early, listening to user frustrations, and communicating insights visually.

If I Had More Time

  • Run A/B tests on multiple layout options
  • Conduct a card-sorting activity for filter categories
  • Expand user testing to include Zara desktop users
"The most valuable insight from this research was understanding that users don't just want new features—they want more control over their existing experience."
