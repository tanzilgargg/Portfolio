Touch Without Sight

Designing a Braille-Inspired Tactile Overlay for Smartphone Accessibility

My Role + Duration

Role

UX Researcher & Prototype Designer

Duration

3 Weeks

Scope

End-to-end research, analysis, ideation, low-fidelity prototyping, and usability testing

Project Overview

This independent project explores how tactile interaction can empower visually impaired users to navigate smartphone interfaces more confidently. The focus was on designing a low-fidelity, Braille-inspired tactile overlay that supports non-visual use of essential phone functions, such as answering calls, sending messages, and accessing voice input—without relying solely on audio feedback or screen readers.

Problem + Goals

Problem

Modern smartphones are built around visual interfaces, which present major usability barriers for blind or low-vision users. Standard accessibility features such as voice commands and screen readers can be effective but often lack reliability, privacy, or ease in public settings.

Project Goals

  • Understand the everyday smartphone navigation challenges faced by blind and low-vision users
  • Identify unmet needs related to non-visual interactions
  • Prototype a tactile, Braille-inspired solution to enhance spatial orientation and task success
  • Test its usability through real-world task performance

Research Plan

Methodologies

  • Semi-structured user interviews
  • Observational usability testing (with and without overlay)
  • Task performance measurement

Participants

  • 3 blind users (ages 24–40)
  • 2 sighted users (blindfolded, for contrast testing)

Key Research Questions

  • How do visually impaired users currently perform tasks like messaging or answering calls?
  • What spatial or tactile strategies do they rely on, if any?
  • How do they perceive and trust audio-based interactions?

Summary of Findings

Quantitative

100%

30%

Qualitative

Participants expressed relief when using tactile cues to orient themselves. Even users unfamiliar with Braille benefited from simple tactile zoning. Audio-only solutions were often avoided in public due to privacy and noise concerns.

Findings

User Quotes

"Without the overlay, I'm constantly second-guessing."

"This gives me a sense of where I am on the screen."

"I don't like talking to my phone in public—it draws attention."

Pain Points

  • Lack of spatial reference on smooth screens
  • High dependency on inconsistent voice input
  • Accidental mis-taps due to ambiguous gestures

Emotional Themes

  • Desire for greater autonomy
  • Fatigue from trial-and-error interaction
  • Preference for discreet, tactile feedback

Insights & Opportunities

Key Insights

  • Tactile orientation improves both usability and user confidence
  • Minimal, consistent spatial cues reduce error rates
  • Users are open to physical overlays if they're portable and unobtrusive

Design Opportunities

  • Create overlays adaptable to multiple devices
  • Explore pairing tactile cues with subtle haptic feedback
  • Expand concept to include kiosk or wearable interfaces

Proposed Solution

Overview

A low-fidelity tactile phone overlay made from flexible craft foam with raised indicators for key zones:

  • Home, Back, Call, Message, Voice Input, Notifications

This overlay creates a non-digital layer of interaction that empowers users to build spatial awareness and muscle memory.

User Flow

  • Unlock phone using tactile "Home" cue
  • Swipe upward from center zone to answer calls
  • Tap top-right corner to activate voice messaging
  • Navigate to apps via side-bump markers

Wireframes

Wireframes can be added to show how tactile areas align with app functions. Each tactile cue maps clearly to a digital action.

Reflection + Lessons Learned

This project reaffirmed that meaningful accessibility doesn't always require advanced technology. Simplicity, physical intuition, and user empathy were central to this design. I learned to test beyond sighted assumptions, and that the most empowering experiences often come from small, thoughtful interventions.

Next Steps

  • 3D print mid-fidelity overlay prototypes
  • Expand testing to include diverse phone models and users
  • Explore collaborations with accessibility-focused organizations for field testing
